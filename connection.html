How They Were Different

The Second Great Awakening and Unitarianism represented two very different approaches to religion in early America. The Second Great Awakening focused on emotional revival meetings and personal conversion experiences, while Unitarianism focused on reason, education, and moral improvement.

How Unitarianism Affected the Awakening

Unitarianism influenced the Second Great Awakening mainly by opposing it. Unitarians criticized revival meetings for being overly emotional and lacking structure. They believed religion should be based on logic and personal growth rather than emotional experiences.

This criticism created a clear divide in American religion. As Unitarianism grew in New England, revivalists in the Second Great Awakening continued to emphasize emotional preaching even more strongly. In this way, the two movements pushed against each other and highlighted different ideas about faith.

Big Idea

Together, these movements show how American religion became more diverse. One side focused on emotion and revival, while the other focused on reason and education. This disagreement helped shape future religious and social reform movements in the United States.
